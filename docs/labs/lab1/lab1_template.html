2.2 Data Quality Assessment

Your Task: Calculate margin of error percentages and create reliability categories.

Requirements: - Calculate MOE percentage: (margin of error / estimate) * 100 - Create reliability categories: - High Confidence: MOE < 5% - Moderate Confidence: MOE 5-10%
- Low Confidence: MOE > 10% - Create a flag for unreliable estimates (MOE > 10%)

Hint: Use mutate() with case_when() for the categories.

# Calculate MOE percentage and reliability categories using mutate()
ca_county_reliability <- clean_data %>%
  mutate(
    moe_pct = (`Median HH Income Margin of Error`/`Median Household Income`)*100,
    category = case_when(
      moe_pct < 5 ~ "High Confidence",
      moe_pct >= 5 & moe_pct <= 10 ~ "Moderate Confidence",
      moe_pct > 10 ~ "Low Confidence"
      ),
    flag = case_when(category == "Low Confidence" ~ "!!!",
                   TRUE ~ "    "  )
)

5.1 Analysis Integration and Professional Summary

Your Task: Write an executive summary that integrates findings from all four analyses.

Executive Summary Requirements: 1. Overall Pattern Identification: What are the systematic patterns across all your analyses? 2. Equity Assessment: Which communities face the greatest risk of algorithmic bias based on your findings? 3. Root Cause Analysis: What underlying factors drive both data quality issues and bias risk? 4. Strategic Recommendations: What should the Department implement to address these systematic issues?

Executive Summary:

The overall analysis focused on the reliability of ACS data for median household income at the county level and racial and ethnic composition at the census-tract level shows a clear understanding of how data reliability varies across measures. Data reliability differs by both variable and geographic or political division. County-level income estimates are mostly reliable, with almost 70 percent of counties providing data where the margin of error falls below 5 percent. In contrast, census-tract-level racial composition data are largely unreliable. For the randomly selected counties, regardless of income data reliability, racial data reliability remained consistently low. Out of 390 census tracts analyzed, all exhibited unreliable racial or ethnic estimates failing the 15% threshold.

Data reliability is especially weak for race and ethnicity variables, primarily because margins of error are high across tracts. This suggests a need to reconsider current data collection approaches. Census tracts in the analysis have relatively small populations, averaging around 4,000 residents. A general inference from the results is that smaller population sizes are associated with higher margins of error. As a result, existing ACS sampling and data collection methods may require revision to produce more reliable tract-level estimates.

The root cause of these issues appears to lie in the sampling design and overall data collection methodology. Capturing a larger effective sample would be necessary to improve reliability. In the long term, reconsidering census tract sizing or adopting alternative aggregation strategies may be necessary to support more stable and reliable data collection in the future.

The Department of Human Services should adopt an uncertainty-aware approach to decision-making by incorporating margins of error directly into analytical and algorithmic processes rather than relying on point estimates alone. Census tract level racial and ethnic composition data should not be used in isolation under current reliability conditions and should instead be aggregated to higher geographies or supplemented with additional data. A tiered framework based on data reliability should guide when automated decisions are appropriate versus when further review is required. Finally, reliability flags and margin of error measures should be clearly documented and carried through all outputs so uncertainty is visible at the point of decision
